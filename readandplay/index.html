HML 2: Lonesome Dove

A dive into my favorite read of 2025 — and maybe ever — Larry McMurtry’s Lonesome Dove: the sprawling Western that transcended its genre, made me laugh, broke my heart, and convinced this skeptic that it more than deserved its Pulitzer.

The Otter • June 17, 2026 • 12 minute read

I only considered reading Lonesome Dove by Larry McMurtry because of its 4.58/5.00 rating across nearly a quarter-million ratings and twenty thousand glowing reviews on Goodreads. On the surface, not much of the book appealed to me: cowboys, cattle, late 1870s, Texas, and what seemed like a cast of characters very dominated by men. An almost 1000 page book (960 pages) that can be summarized in one sentence: In the American West of the late 1870s, a group of boys led by two problematic middle-aged ex-Texas rangers attempt to drive an out-of-their-depth sized herd of stolen cattle up to Montana and be one of the first people to settle there.

But as a big stats guy, I felt a strong obligation to give it a go. And wow, nearly a thousand pages later, it is quite possibly the best book I have ever read.

To be fair, the Goodreads summary does a better job making the book sound more appealing: “A love story, an adventure, and an epic of the frontier. Journey to the dusty little Texas town of Lonesome Dove and meet an unforgettable assortment of heroes and outlaws, whores and ladies, Indians and settlers. Richly authentic, beautifully written, always dramatic, Lonesome Dove is a book to make us laugh, weep, dream, and remember.”

With that said, in no way am I conceding that my one sentence summary is misleading or incomplete. It really is just that. I remember reading even the Goodreads introduction and thinking: can one be any more boring and vague? Ok, we have ourselves another “great Western,” but after reading the book, boy, is Lonesome Dove a “richly authentic, beautifully written adventure story that made me laugh, weep, dream, and remember.” Countless times, I had to put the book down and really reflect and think about what I was thinking and what exactly I was feeling. It felt like I experienced forty-seven types of “it brought tears to my eyes”: tears welled from joy, bittersweetness, warmth, empathy, sympathy, sadness, regret, discomfort, confusion, understanding, awe, nostalgia, shock, you name it.

When I put the book down for the last time, I felt like I had just said goodbye to some of my best friends. I felt overwhelming satisfaction and gratitude.

To begin, one of my favorite parts about Lonesome Dove and perhaps its greatest strength is that it contains characters that feel more real than those in any other book I have ever read. In many ways they feel more real than even real people, like humanity personified.

Often, stories worth telling, fiction or non-fiction, are of extraordinary people in extraordinary situations doing extraordinary things. And rightfully so. We love stories that push the limits of humanity or the human experience. Stories surrounding issues so deep-rooted in society that it takes someone special to come along to begin to tackle and resolve them, to give us hope that one day, a person could really come along and do so. Stories of conquests and victories, heroes and heroines, greatness leading to success against all odds.

In Lonesome Dove, it felt plausible, even probable, that I could meet any one of them on the streets or in my daily life. Every character has their strengths and flaws and throughout the book made frustrating decisions that are ultimately completely consistent with their character. There was always reason for their strengths and flaws, fears, reluctances and courage, arrogance, confidence, and timidness. They make decisions I don’t agree with, sometimes misaligned with my personal values, and often say things I wouldn’t say myself, but at the end of the day, I loved and adored them all. And they made sense. Nothing in the book felt like it didn’t belong. It all contributed to the depth of the characters: their histories, their thoughts, their actions, reactions.

This depth allowed me to do what I feel we don’t get to do with real people we encounter in our lives — to truly understand each and every one of them. Truly understanding the motivations behind the characters’ choices made it easy for me to empathize, no matter how much I might dislike or disapprove of what they said or did. Sometimes, I’m upset, but I’m rarely angry. In many books, characters make decisions that move the plot. In Lonesome Dove, things just happen, and the characters make decisions because of who they are. Their story and their journey moves at a pace no one controls. In Lonesome Dove, few characters, if any, get happy endings. The story keeps going even when you can’t keep going with it.

Furthermore, what amazes me is how seamless McMurtry lets us in on each of the characters. These days, multiple POV books that give characters depth by allocating their own chapters and diving into their thoughts and motivation is not uncommon and often very effective. I feel that a part of Lonesome Dove that makes it so special is how McMurtry achieves this level of intimacy for the entire cast without defaulting to multiple POVs. The prose flows so freely from character to character with startling fluidity, drifting from a person’s thoughts to another’s silent reaction within the same page, and often even the same paragraph. It doesn’t make me feel like I’m at a campfire with just any one character at a time. It makes me feel like I am simply at the campfire.

In Lonesome Dove, where this really shines is in the deaths throughout the book. As with many adventures, death seems inevitable, commonplace, and that is no different for that of the Hat Creek Cattle Company. The depictions of how each individual of the group reacts to their fallen comrades and the thoughts they go through feel so true and consistent with their characters, personalities, and relationships with those who would not continue on with them. In addition, it never seemed like the deaths occurred in dramatic fashion. Characters died because their journey is dangerous and the balance that McMurtry struck with each character between acceptance, delusion, and immense grief felt real.

This is especially clear in one devastating loss late in the novel, when the company is forced to confront what one of its quietest members had meant to them all along. He is one of the most competent people in the book: steady, observant, helpful in nearly every situation, and almost entirely without ego. He never demands recognition, and because of that, he is easy for the others to overlook in the daily rhythm of the journey. But when he is gone, the men are left with the painful realization that they had not only depended on him, but loved him. Several of them seem struck by the same regret: that they had admired him, trusted him, and relied on him, but had never quite made him a friend in the way they realized they wanted and the way he deserved.

My two criticisms of the book are the underrepresentation of central female characters and the largely stereotypical portrayal of Native American and Mexican characters, many of whom are depicted as violent, threatening, or villainous with little reason and little to no interiority. The main characters’ perspectives may partially explain this: much of the world is filtered through men shaped by violence, prejudice, fear, and frontier myth. But even allowing for that, the Native American and Mexican characters feel far less varied and fully realized than the rest of the cast, including secondary members of the Hat Creek Cattle Company. In a novel otherwise so rich with personality, contradiction, and emotional depth, that limitation stands out.

That said, while there are only two prominent female characters throughout the book, one of whom we don’t meet until the last third, I absolutely loved their stories. For much of the book, we only get to see Lonesome Dove, the band of misfit cowboys, and the world McMurtry has built through one female character, Lorie, who starts out as the town’s prostitute. Though the ideas Lonesome Dove explores through its female characters often feel secondary and are far from the novel’s most profound element, they still stood out to me. Perhaps because the absence of female voices felt so stark throughout the book, I found myself paying especially close attention whenever their perspectives came to the forefront. In the end, I was surprised by how much their stories affected me.

Through Lorie’s narrow and sheltered perspective, we see an ugliness in the world of Lonesome Dove that is impossible to ignore. She is the only woman in an overwhelmingly male-dominated setting, and as a result, she becomes less a person to many of the men around her and more an object of desire, fantasy, pity, protection, or possession. What makes her story tragic is that her naivete, especially early on, is not simple foolishness, but the product of a life made small by circumstance. There are moments when she seems capable of becoming so much more, when glimpses of strength, stubbornness, and fire break through, only for the world, and men, around her to close back in. Unlike many of the men, whose lives have been shaped by travel, work, and years of wandering, Lorie has had little chance to know the world beyond what men have allowed her to see, and that narrowness gives her worldview a heartbreaking innocence. This is revealed through her understanding of love and care throughout the novel, which has been shaped by a world that has offered her so little of either. Even the relationships she reflects on with warmth feel painfully lacking from the outside, but she appreciates them because, in many ways, she has never been given reason to expect more.

As the story goes on, perhaps unsurprisingly, Lorie suffers more than almost anyone, enduring not only neglect and dismissal, but also sexual violence of unbearable brutality. It makes sense, then, that she retreats toward the only man who seems to treat her with any measure of gentleness and respect. Whether he truly treats her as well as she believes is more complicated. He protects her, comforts her, and loves her in his way, but protection is not the same as equality, and comfort is not the same as commitment. Part of what makes their relationship so moving and so sad is that her need for safety becomes tangled with love. Maybe she confuses the two; maybe she really does love him. Maybe both are true. And he loves her too, but it never felt like enough. Not fully. Not in the way she needs, and not in the way he has loved before. But how could he, when he will never see her as his equal? And yet, I still loved their relationship.

Lorie’s story is complemented and contrasted by one of my favorite characters in the entire novel: Clara. We do not meet her until the beginning of the novel’s third and final section, and our time with her is relatively brief, but Clara’s presence is immediate and magnetic. Within pages, I understood why Gus has carried her with him for decades. I understood how she could leave such a permanent mark on him, how she could soften the hard, sarcastic, and egotistical exterior of the great Augustus McCrae. All of a sudden, Gus goes from someone most of us admire to someone we can relate to at a fundamental level: a boy who fell in love with a sharp, warm, clear-eyed, witty girl. The Clara we meet is that girl and much more: emotionally guarded by responsibility, hardened by difficult choices, and shaped by a life that has demanded practicality over sentiment. Somehow, McMurtry gives Clara such gravity that everyone around her seems to feel it. They notice her, respond to her, orbit her.

When Gus and Clara are finally together, the mood becomes a unique blend of mellowness, electricity, and restlessness. The chapters where they finally interact in private are among my favorite scenes I have read—ever. Their conversations carry the weight of an entire life that could have been: years of enduring and evolving affection, longing, regret, and understanding, all hovering at the tips of their tongues but never fully expressed. They feel like intimate small talk charged with small, restrained smiles that ooze understanding, glances that say a thousand words, and reluctant hugs that carry a lifetime of emotion. If my words have not done these scenes justice, perhaps this is most telling: while reading those few chapters, I found myself putting the book down again and again, taking deep breaths, and simply sitting with what I had just read.

Clara’s impact does not stop there: her entrance also marks the beginning of Lorie’s recovery and growth. Lorie herself cannot quite explain why she feels a shift in her energy, and I am not sure I can fully find the words either. But to me, it feels entirely believable that simply being in the presence of a woman like Clara — and being welcomed by her — would be transformative. When Lorie chooses to leave the secure and familiar protection of Gus, who helped her survive the worst of what she endured, to stay with Clara, a woman she has only just met, the decision is surprising, but deeply moving. It feels like one of the first choices she makes not out of fear, dependence, or survival, but out of a fragile new belief that another life might be available to her.

In Clara, Lorie sees not just safety, but hope. In a novel so dominated by men that its world could feel almost confining at times, it was refreshing and moving to see something as foundational as a young woman being inspired by another woman carry so much weight. Clara’s life, difficult as it has been, has still given her some room for choice, love, work, family, and independence — things Lorie has rarely had the chance to claim for herself. So when Lonesome Dove’s two most prominent female characters find strength, purpose, and a way forward in each other, the moment feels monumental.

Every time I think I’ve gotten to the most emotional part of the story, we are hit with countless other gut punches. Our beloved characters’ struggles with closure, loss, acceptance continue all the way to the end. For Call, for Gus, for Deets, for Newt.

Man, Lonesome Dove.

It took me two weeks to read the first 200 or so pages, and about four days, during a regular workweek, to read the next 800. The weekend that followed was spent sitting by the lake, thinking about starting a new book, but unable to leave that world behind just yet.

There were moments when I could almost see the flat plains reflected on the lake’s still surface, the cattle kicking up swirls of dust under the beating, unforgiving sun. Gus on his horse, galloping at a playful, almost inviting pace. Call glancing over at him, then very subtly rolling his eyes. Newt’s nervous energy. Deets’s smile, far ahead, as he scouts somewhere out in the distance. Dish’s mild frustration and perpetual heartbreak. Pea Eye’s endearing cluelessness. The pigs. Bolivar. Po Campo. Lorie. The Irishmen. I’d look the other way and see July, Joe, Roscoe, and Janey. Wilbarger off in the distance.

Lonesome Dove has so much of what I love in a story: adventure, humor, heartbreak, romance, friendship, loss, and growth. It is a book I will remember for a very long time, and one I am sure will be even better the second time around. Yes, there will be a second time. This book touched my heart in a way I did not know literature could. It made me laugh, made me angry, made me cry, and, more than anything, made me grateful that I got to go on this adventure with them.
