K2: More of The Trolley Problem

A dive into vartiations of the infamous trolley problem, its origin, and a brief discussion on its relevance in AI ethics.

The Otter • June 29, 2026 • 4 minute read

More likely than not, we’ve all heard of the trolley problem.

First, the classic: imagine a trolley barreling down its tracks, on its way to killing five people tied to the rails. You have access to a lever that will switch the trolley onto another track, where only one person is tied down. Do you pull the lever?

What if, instead, you are standing on a bridge above the tracks next to a large man? The only way to stop the trolley from hitting the five tied-up people is to push the man off the bridge and onto the tracks. His weight will stop the trolley, but he will die. Do you push him onto the tracks?

On the surface, it is the same problem: do we sacrifice one life to save five? Yet, studies show a significant behavioral shift. In the lever scenario, the majority say they would pull it. In the footbridge scenario, the vast majority refuse to push the man—especially when the former question does not precede the latter.

Often introduced as a gateway to a discussion on ethics and morality, the problem can feel relevant to any discipline.

For example, back in the Fall of 2022, I took a computer science seminar class that included an AI ethics component where the trolley problem came up via a reading of the paper Never Mind the Trolley: The Ethics of Autonomous Vehicles in Mundane Situations, written by Johannes Himmelreich of Syracuse in 2018.

The discourse surrounding AI ethics has been around for a long time, but it has grown increasingly prominent over the past decade as autonomous vehicles become a reality and the prospect of Artificial General Intelligence looms closer. For a casual read on the topic, consider Gary Marcus’ 2012 piece published in The New Yorker: Moral Machines.

In the autonomous vehicle setting, imagine a scenario where a self-driving car is approaching a tunnel when a person suddenly appears in its path. A collision is unavoidable, and there are only two options: either the car runs over the pedestrian, killing them, or the car swerves into the wall of the tunnel, killing the passenger. What should the car do?

Does our answer change if there were two passengers? Or two pedestrians? Or children? Should it?

While I’ve always known of the trolley problem, learning about it in detail has transformed it into one of my favorite philosophical discussions—and naturally, one of my favorite road trip conversation starters. It challenges and exposes the uncomfortable inconsistencies in how we process right and wrong and highlights the tug-of-war between our assessment of outcomes and our boundaries regarding how those outcomes are achieved.

It seems this struggle—and the question of whether the ends justify the means—will forever remain timeless. This conflict can be distilled into two philosophical ideas: Utilitarianism and Deontology. Utilitarianism asks us to judge actions by their consequences: maximize well-being, minimize suffering. Deontology, by contrast, insists that some actions are wrong regardless of outcome, especially when they treat human beings merely as tools for achieving some greater good.

Since the original dilemma, many variations of the trolley problem have been introduced to test our moral boundaries.

An additional layer to the problem is that of authority. Certainly, in the standing on the bridge scenario, we are not authorized to push the man onto the tracks as that would undoubtedly constitute murder while pulling the lever may not.

What if, instead of being a bystander with the power to pull the lever or push the man, you were the trolley driver? What if you were a train station operator and ordinarily, part of your job was to pull levers and plan train paths? In either case, do you have the authority and moral obligation to steer it towards the tracks with one person and not five people?

In an even more extreme example, imagine you are a doctor. You are monitoring the lives of five people, all of whom need an organ transplant. Two need a lung, two need kidneys, and another needs a heart. You happen to be working in the emergency room that day, and a person walks in with a broken arm; he had just fallen off his bike, but otherwise, he is completely healthy. In the operating room, while putting screws in his arm, a thought comes to you: what if we take his lungs, kidneys, and heart to save the five people in critical condition? Do you do it? How is it a different question than the trolley problem? In this case, are you the trolley driver, the bystander, or neither?

Other variations include: a group of spelunkers deciding whether to dynamite a companion stuck in the only exit from a flooding cave, a judge hanging an apparently blameless suspect to save more innocents from a bloody-minded mob, a pilot whose airplane is going down choosing to aim for a more or less populated area.

How do we choose?

Interestingly, the trolley problem actually originated in a 1967 essay on the morality of abortion by Philippa Foot. Yes, we’ve been talking about abortion (and these dilemmas) for a long time. She was pretty adamant that the driver should not hesitate to redirect the tram, whereas the doctor and the judge should not take the life of an innocent person—though she was not so sure what to conclude regarding abortion.

For a detailed philosophical discussion on the trolley problem, consider reading Frances Myrna Kamm’s The Trolley Problem Mysteries.

I leave you with this very real question: How do we begin to “teach” artificial intelligence morals and ethics if, throughout history, we ourselves have never been able to perfectly articulate them? It is undeniable that we are building systems that will increasingly act in morally significant ways. Is it sufficient to train these systems on massive amounts of human data and hope that the result reflects the general morality of humanity? Yes or no, that seems to be the direction we’re heading.

K1: Greece, Eurozone's Annoying Sibling Who Refused to Grow Up

How Greece gave the EU back pain.

The Otter • July 1, 2025 • 4 minute read

... from having to carry it so hard all these years.

How I got here: One of my good friends is traveling through Europe. The other day she complained about how train delays and misschedules made her routine two hour trip from Madrid to Valencia a seven hour endeavor. Instead of arriving at 11 pm, she arrived at 4 am. Oof.

That got me thinking: Europe → Incompetency → Greece (sorry Greece). Over the past few decades, I’ve occasionally, but consistently, heard about Greece’s financial chaos in the context of the Eurozone and the EU. I guess that’s why my train of thought ended up there. The brain works in mysterious ways. So I decided to dig deeper.

Here’s a little bit about the European Union, the Eurozone, and how Greece fits into the picture.

First, let’s talk about a clear key identifying feature between the European Union (EU) and the Eurozone. The EU (27 countries) is a group of countries in Europe where people, money, and goods move freely with minimal restrictions. Think of it as a shared neighborhood with no locks and open doors. The Eurozone (20 countries) is the group of EU countries that all use the same currency: the Euro.

Side Nugget: Passport controls have been abolished for travel within the Schengen Area (29 countries) as an element within the wider area of freedom, security and justice (AFSJ) policy of the EU. It mostly works like a single country for international travel purposes — one visa, one border check, lots of movement.

Another Nugget: The EU has a long and intricate history, dating back to the post–World War II years. Its official founding moment came in 1993, with the Maastricht Treaty (aka the Treaty on European Union).

Yet Another Nugget: The Eurozone was launched on January 1st, 1999 by 11 founding members. There could have — maybe should have — been 12, but the 12th didn’t make the cut. Why? Failed some economic tests (officially: the Maastricht criteria). Yup, you guessed it: Greece. After tightening things up, Greece passed a year later and officially joined in 2001 as the 12th member.

Yet Another One: The EU has its own observer seat at the United Nations (UN), but each of its 27 member countries is a full UN member with its own seat and vote. So, the EU gets to talk, but not vote. Hello? Why?

Anyway, Greece. Here's a short ramble — but a pretty juicy one.

So, like I said, the Eurozone club kicked off in 1999 with 11 members. Greece wasn’t allowed in because they didn’t meet the Maastricht criteria — a set of economic rules designed to keep the whole system stable. Understandably, Greece had been struggling with debt and poor fiscal discipline since the 1990s. Naturally, they felt left out. So how did they turn things around so quickly, meet the criteria, and join just two years later in 2001?

Simple. They lied. Classic little sibling move. They lied… to the EU. Bonkers.

After joining the Eurozone frenzy in 01, they felt more secure and overspent even more, especially towards… the 2004 Olympics. By the mid-2000s, its debt-to-GDP ratio (basically how much a country owes compared to how much it earns) was over 100%.

Then came the 2008 global financial crisis. Of course, Greece got exposed. Hard. The lies came back to haunt them. No one wanted to lend to them anymore, and they were shut out of the European bond market. They couldn’t borrow money? They couldn’t pay anyone back.

Quick Detour: A financial default happens when someone — a person, company, or government — fails to pay back a loan on time. That’s what Greece was facing.

Growing restless, the EU’s big siblings swooped in to help: 3 bail outs totalling 260 billion euros. Imagine you mess up real bad and your family just throws money at you. Isn’t that great. Of course, there were harsh regulations on how they can spend it.

There were strict regulations, budget cuts, and tax hikes. Greece’s economy shrank by 25% between 2008 and 2013 — one of the worst post-war recessions in Europe. Unemployment hit 27%, youth unemployment over 50%. Public services were gutted. And their debt-to-GDP ratio? It peaked at 170%, one of the highest in the world.

Politically? Chaos. Greece cycled through frequent elections, radical parties gained traction, and the streets were filled with protests, strikes, and occasional violence. Negotiations with the EU were front-page news for years — and it exposed deep tensions between European countries over what solidarity really means.

Then came the ultimate “bruh” moment:

In 2015, Greece basically said “idgaf” about the bailout rules and… the EU hit back with even tougher ones.

Where’s Aristotle and Co. when you need them? I thought the Greeks were supposed to be smart.

Just when they were crawling out… COVID hit. As a tourism-reliant country, that really messed them up too.

Rip to the Eurozone’s Annoying Sibling Who Refuses to Grow Up. Rip Greece.

Over the past few years, they have been doing better though. Post pandemic, there’s been a surge in tourism, rising foreign investment, and EU recovery funds. Unemployment has gradually declined, and Greece even returned to investment-grade status in 2023 for the first time in over a decade — showing signs they might be able to take care of themselves.

However, challenges remain: public debt is still high, inflation has pressured households, and structural reforms are ongoing. Still, by post-crisis standards, Greece’s economic outlook is more stable and optimistic than it’s been in years.
