SS1: The Spaces Left Open

A story of heartbreak, neglected emotions, and unspoken feelings: Not for the first time, but perhaps for the last, when Gus feels like Alice puts him second once again, he is forced to decide whether to remain in a perpetual state of emotional turmoil or walk away from his best friend.

The Otter • Jun 6, 2026 • 25 minute read

"For all of our infinite wisdom, feelings (seem to) reign supreme."

The other day, Alice and Gus got into an argument over the phone. It wasn't really a fight, but it may well be one that ends their friendship—or whatever it is they've become to each other. Gus was on his way over to Mark's apartment to pick up his bike for the summer while Mark was out of town for a few months.

It was a beautiful, sunny day. As a warm, gentle breeze swept across Gus’ face, he was reminded of a similar afternoon a year ago, when Alice and he had gone rollerblading. Or rather, when Alice had gone rollerblading while holding onto Gus’ arm as he biked ahead, dragging her along behind him. Gus smiled, reached into his right shorts pocket, and pulled out his phone.

“You know what we’d be doing right now if you were in Chicago?” he texted Alice. “Rollerblading.”

This time last year, Alice and Gus were as close as they had ever been and perhaps ever would be. He was soon leaving for a conference in Seattle, before flying straight to Vietnam to visit his parents. Alice was leaving Chicago for a new job in Kentucky, and she would be gone before he made it back.

That week, as in other times when they knew they’d be separated for a while, they became inseparable, savoring their last days together, clinging to every shared moment. But this time it felt more intense than ever before; they didn’t know when they would see each other again.

A couple seconds later, she facetimed with a huge smile on her face, “Whatcha up to?”

“Hello there,” he said flatly, with his patented smirk, as her smile broke into a beaming grin. “I’m on my way to pick up Mark’s bike. He’s letting me borrow it for the summer.”

“Oh cool! That’s nice of him.”

“Yeah. I heard you’re going to California next week.” His voice turned bright, almost too bright—excited, but with a quiet edge of anxiety as he waited to hear more about the trip. And so it began. One of the few times in recent memory where he had no control over his emotions. His reaction and judgement were as transparent as he ever remembered them being.

Alice and Gus had always had a complicated relationship. Or probably more accurately, Gus had always had a complicated relationship with Alice.

Over the few months leading up to that day, Alice and Gus had been calling each other quite frequently—about two to three times a week, up from once every couple of weeks before that. In many of those calls, she had talked about the possibility of them traveling together over the summer, just as they had the year before. She said she had set alerts for cheap flights to Vietnam and that she was excited about the prospect of meeting his family. She always emphasized that, if booked well in advance, the flights would be much cheaper. He told her he would love to travel with her—always, anywhere, especially to Vietnam.

But during that time, his main priority was preparing for and passing his qualifying exams for grad school. After that, he planned to head to Philadelphia the week following his quals for his brother’s graduation, and to spend time with his parents who were traveling in for it. Beyond that, he needed to confirm his travel plans with his research advisor, who was funding him over the summer, the most important of which was going home to see his family, a trip she would, of course, be welcome to join. Gus didn’t yet know when any of it would happen, though; his focus had to stay on his quals, with everything else still uncertain. He made sure she understood and of course, she did.

In these conversations, Gus always asked Alice when during the summer would be best for travel. As a grad student, he worked a lot. Gus always thought grad students often exaggerate how much they work, but they probably still work more than most. However, with that comes the flexibility to take time off whenever they want. She, on the other hand, would have to plan around work and use PTO. How many days could she realistically take? When would it make most sense for her to step away?

Alice said she could probably manage up to five days of PTO at a time, but it would be tricky in the summer. At Pepsico, the “100 days of summer” are their busiest period. If she did take time off, she’d need to let her managers know well in advance.

For the next few months, Gus and Alice’s conversations were filled with places they wanted to see, foods they wanted to try, and cultures they wanted to experience. He was excited for the trip, for himself, for them. But really, he felt that much of that excitement was for her. Alice was the least well-traveled member of their friend group, but also, he thought, the person most likely to greet the unfamiliar with genuine joy, curiosity, and appreciation. Since they’ve met, Gus had introduced her to so many new foods and cultures, and in return, she had introduced him to traditions and ways of seeing the world he might never have discovered without her. Gus was excited to watch Alice’s horizons widen, to see her step beyond the familiar edges of the Midwest and discover just how much more there was to love.

Just imagining Alice’s excitement—skipping through the shimmering golden gates of Asian temples, or leaning over a bowl of street noodles, eyes wide as she took in the fusion of flavors and aromas, old and new—was enough to make Gus’ heart quicken.

A few days before the upsetting call, Gus told Alice that he had scored a couple of half-off tickets to Hawaii, so that could be an option too. They could take a few days off, eat fresh poke, surf, and spend a long weekend above the clouds, looking out from the slopes of volcanoes over the endless blue of the Pacific. His college roommate of three years, Max, would be there all summer and would have been a fantastic, more-than-willing host. Alice said she thought that sounded awesome.

A few days later, the day before the call, Gus played tennis with Jack, a mutual friend of his and Alice’s, who would be interning at Google in the Bay Area that summer. Jack mentioned, almost in passing, that Alice had told him she would be in California for a week or so, right around the time he would start work there. Jack seemed surprised when Gus told him he had no idea. Until that moment, Gus hadn’t known any of it was even in the works.

“Must be for work, I guess,” Gus said, though even as he said it, the explanation felt thin. “But why would work take her all the way out to California?”

And why hadn’t Alice mentioned it to him?

“I don’t know,” Jack said. “She texted me a few days ago. Said she was going to San Jose, and that maybe we should meet up.”

Jack and Alice never ended up seeing each other, despite his attempts to schedule something.

San Jose.

There’s no way. It couldn’t be.

Alice had been seeing a guy down in Kentucky that fall, James, an assistant basketball coach at UK, and it had ended badly. James was from San Jose. Gus didn’t know the full story though because, apparently, although Alice and Gus were best friends, they were not the kind who talked about things like that. To Gus, it always stung that Alice didn’t seem to want to share, but it was what it was. For a month or so leading up to their breakup, Alice had ranted about James to him. Then, gradually, James faded out of their conversations. Naturally, Gus assumed he was out of the picture.

Yeah. It couldn’t be about him.

“Oh yeah! James is going home for a couple weeks, and he invited me to stay with him for one of them!” Alice exclaimed through the phone.

“James?” Gus said. “I didn’t know you guys still spoke.”

Much less hung out. Much less were comfortable enough to take a whole trip together.

“Oh yeah. James and I are chill. We’ve been chill.”

Oh.

He tried to keep his voice casual. “Uh, okay. How many days of PTO did you take for the trip?”

What he really meant was: how many days does that leave for us?

“Five days. Since Memorial Day weekend is coming up, I thought I’d take a whole week off and go to California for a bit.”

“You’re going to California for ten days?” he asked, his eyebrows tightening into something between confusion and accusation.

A flicker of hurt crossed Alice’s face, mixed with something like disbelief. “Yeah.”

Gus didn’t know if he missed the hurt in her expression, or if he saw it and was simply too stunned to soften himself. All he could do was blurt out, “California.”

“With James.”

The words landed badly. Gus could hear it even as he said them: the disbelief, the mockery, the condescension.

A frown settled across Alice’s face, and color seemed to leave her all at once. They fell silent for what felt like an eternity. Her face filled his phone, his filled hers, and for a few awful seconds, they simply stared at each other while a million thoughts tore through his mind.

“You’re going to California for ten days…”

Another pause. The corners of Alice’s mouth sank lower, her lips parting slightly as she absorbed the judgment coming from him—unfair, uninvited, and unmistakable.

“But what about Vietnam? Japan? China? Portugal, Spain, Italy, Morocco? Hawaii? Chile? Ten days. You could go anywhere, and you’re going to California?”

Gus kept piling on, driven by disbelief, clearly not himself—or maybe, more frighteningly, more himself than he wanted to admit.

“I don’t know what to say,” Alice said. “He invited me. I said yes.”

More silence.

“Anyway,” Gus said, trying to drag them somewhere safer, “what do you have going on tonight?”

Alice turned back to the mirror and reluctantly resumed putting on her makeup. “Just going out with Hannah.”

Gus and Alice tried to catch up about other things, but the conversation had gone cold. Lifeless. Nothing like their usual playful, energetic back-and-forth. Instead, they became two strangers making small talk in the ruins of something neither of them knew how to name. Soon after, as he approached Mark’s apartment, he blurted, “Well, alright. I’m pulling up to Mark’s, so I’ll talk to you soon.”

“Sounds good. Bye,” she said, and hung up immediately.

No usual “Love you, bye.” that Gus had grown so accustomed to. No sweet smile before the screen went dark. On any other call, the absence of those words would have been glaring. It would have left a small hollow in his chest, one he would have carried until the next time her name lit up his phone. Not that he had ever had to carry it before. Alice had always said it without fail.

But, he was so upset he didn’t think he even noticed.

As Gus slipped his phone back into his shorts pocket and approached the steps to Mark’s apartment, he stopped dead in his tracks and looked up, staring into nothing. First came numbness. Then guilt, sudden and overwhelming.

What had he done?

Alice had been dreaming about travel for so long, and now that she was finally getting to go somewhere, probably more excited than she had been about anything in a long time, he had taken that joy and turned it into something ugly. She had tried to share her excitement with him, and he had answered with negativity, judgment, and condescension.

He took a few steps toward the entrance, already forming an apology in his head, then stopped and sat down on the pavement.

“sorry, that was terrible behavior by me, im glad youre going on a trip you feel good and excited about, i was pretty surprised by it all and also just by the circumstances honestly, im sorry. talk soon.” Gus tapped the send icon, watched the big blue bubble pop up, followed closely by the delivered tag under it, and looked up at the house across the street, but saw nothing. He meant it, but they felt like empty words compared to the harsh and exceedingly transparent view on the matter he had expressed just seconds ago.

Instead of going straight home, Gus decided to go for a long bike ride. Forty-five minutes later, he was riding down a lush green road, past classic American houses tucked beneath towering trees. The sun was setting, cool gusts of wind rushed over him, and the smell of spring brushed against his face. Then he felt a buzz against his thigh.

It was Alice.

“I’m honestly pretty upset about it. It’s free accommodation, I know the person, and I was really excited about it. I didn’t feel like I should’ve had to fully explain why it mattered to me or why it’s probably the smarter financial decision for me right now.”

Twenty minutes farther down the road, with an emptiness in his chest and guilt that felt like it would never leave, Gus responded, “i know. im sorry.”

As he pedaled, he sank deeper into thought and frustration beneath the perpetual hum of insects and the soft flutter of summer moths that had begun a couple of weeks earlier.

For a long time, Gus loved her. Gun to his head, he would probably still have to say that he did, as much as he ever had. But if Alice asked him out tomorrow, would he say yes? No. It had been no for a while.

Was there some jealousy in knowing she was taking this trip with James? A hint, a splash, maybe even a wave? Sure. But if he was being honest with himself, jealousy was not what swallowed him that evening. It was the feeling of a massive missed opportunity: not his, but hers. The sense that someone he cared about deeply was choosing something familiar when the whole world had opened in front of her.

By most standards, Gus would say he was at least fairly well traveled: over twenty states, over twenty countries. He knew how much every place he had been had stayed with him, and he knew Alice. He knew how exhilarated—borderline euphoric—she would have been somewhere truly unfamiliar, somewhere that made the world feel bigger the moment she stepped into it.

Choosing California felt massively underwhelming compared to everything they had talked about.

And for ten days.

Ten days opens up the whole world. A week? Maybe not. A week is tight. A week means fighting through jet lag and losing whole days to airports and long flights. But if anyone would have been willing to do it—and somehow pull it off without compromising the trip—it would have been Alice.

But ten days? Ten days is different. Ten days is Paris, Florence, and Rome. Barcelona, Lisbon, and Marrakech. Chile and Argentina. Taipei, Hong Kong, Tokyo, and Seoul. Ten days is enough time for the trip of a lifetime.

And she was choosing San Jose.

Gus didn’t like the word hate, but he hated lost opportunities. No—he deeply disliked lost opportunities. And this one made him really, really sad. A little bit because she wasn’t going with him, but he didn’t think it was that. It wasn’t even because she was going with James. But because the whole world was at her fingertips, and she had settled for San Jose.

Also, the financial argument? In preparation for Alice exploring the world, Gus had advised her to open a new travel credit card account so she could start racking up points for free travel. And he knew she had a boatload of points by then. A flight from Chicago to the Bay was well within the same price range as a flight to Lisbon. In miles, Europe might actually have been cheaper.

Later, Gus remembered that Northern California is actually pretty darn cool. People from all over the world travel to the Bay Area, San Francisco, Yosemite, the Pacific Coast. He had forgotten—or maybe conveniently overlooked—how incredible California could be because he had lived there for three years himself.

But still. For ten days?

Why not take a couple of days off, add Memorial Day weekend, and go for five? That way, the door to a bigger trip somewhere else that summer wouldn’t be closed.

That said, free accommodations? Sure. But Gus knew how expensive gas was out there. During Covid, he remembered a friend telling him he drove forty-five minutes out of town for gas because it was seven dollars a gallon there, but eight-fifty in downtown San Francisco. California is massive. You can’t really do a trip of that scale while sleeping in the same place every night. And sure, gas wasn’t that expensive anymore, but it still wasn’t the Midwest.

“In Europe, say hello to cheap hostels, actual rail infrastructure, and Ryanair. His point was, if finances were the bottleneck, a trip to Europe probably wasn’t any more expensive than a trip out West,” he thought.

But even as he thought it, he could hear how much it sounded like an excuse.

As the sun crept beneath the horizon, the familiar sound of the creek rose ahead of him: a rhythmic babble, water trickling gently over smooth pebbles. He slowed as the bike lane became a path along the water. Probably time to turn back. Gus was starting to feel exhausted, though he couldn’t tell whether it was the ride catching up to him, or the weight of everything he had been carrying in his head.

Why hadn’t Alice mentioned the California trip when he brought up Hawaii a few days earlier?

Over the years, Gus had tried his very best to treat her as a best friend, and only a best friend. He felt like he had done a good job. He drew a clear line and never crossed it. But from his end, he often felt like Alice didn’t treat him like one. She didn’t tell him about dates, plans, or boy troubles the way she seemed to tell other people. With him, those stories felt withheld, almost like mentioning another guy would be some kind of confession. Like saying it out loud might shatter what they had—like there was something to break. She had always been adamant that she didn’t want anything more than friendship. So why did it feel like she avoided telling him these things not because they didn’t matter, but because they mattered too much?

Gus wanted to be trusted with those stories the way any of Alice’s friends were, let alone her best friend. Instead, he kept finding out secondhand, usually with a sting that felt embarrassingly close to betrayal, unworthiness—and with the twisted, dangerous logic that maybe some part of her did see him the same way he viewed her.

Along those same lines, Alice constantly expressed appreciation and love for him in words and gestures that made their line feel less like a boundary and more like something they were both pretending not to lean against. She had always been firm about not wanting more, and yet she had agreed to the marriage pact he had floated half-jokingly a while back.

That was one of his favorite days he ever spent with her.

It was one of those first truly sunny days, when the heat carried the promise of summer after weeks of being teased with spring, only for the gods to serve them more cold, wet rain. It came during one of those stretches when Gus and Alice both knew they were about to be apart for a while: she was leaving for spring break in Cabo, Mexico, then heading to Disney World in Orlando and on a cruise to the Bahamas with her aunt.

Gus and Alice were walking along a narrow path through a local botanical garden when she said, “You know, this may be the first place I’ve ever been where I could actually imagine myself getting married.”

That took him by surprise. Some small, reckless part of him wondered whether the thought had come to her because she was there with him. She had always had impossibly high standards when it came to romance, proposals, and especially weddings. For a while, he had been ready to meet and exceed them all. That was when he brought up the marriage pact as a joke.

“Really? Thirty? That feels too young for a marriage pact.” he asked, bemused.

“Yeah, I’m thinking thirty.” Alice responded, warmly but quite flirtily, casually turning toward him with a small smile that, to Gus, outshone all the flowers blooming around them.

Gus knew Alice wanted him to meet her eyes, but all he could do was face forward and try to contain the ridiculous peace and joy that her smile had stirred in him. He failed. He was beaming, and eventually, inevitably, he turned toward her. They made eye contact, both of them giggling and squinting beneath the bright sun.

Gus knew marriage pacts were nothing-burgers. No one should build a future around those innocent little promises friends make when the moment feels warm enough to let them. Heck, he had one with his ex, whom he had broken up with amicably a few years earlier.

But in that moment, when she said thirty—not forty, not forty-five—something in him gave way. It felt real. It felt possible. He wanted to reach over and hold her hand, to pull her into him, maybe even kiss her. They were thoughts Gus rarely, if ever, let himself touch. But as always, he stayed firmly on his side of the line.

“But like, would you be happy about it?” he asked. “Or would you feel like you were settling?”

“I would be very, very happy,” she said. “Gus, you make me very happy. And honestly, outside of family, you are the closest I’ve ever felt to anyone.”

They walked into a small shaded pavilion, and he sat down, staring ahead at the flowering shrubs that seemed to wall them off from the rest of the world. Gus tried not to hold on to Alice’s words too tightly, afraid that if he let them settle too deeply, he wouldn’t know how to let them go. She sat beside him and looked forward too. For a moment, they were in their own little world, both of them still, as if moving might disturb whatever fragile thing had settled between them.

As usual, his curiosity took over. Or maybe he was just reaching for more.

“But you dated your ex for five years.”

“Yes. I know. Him included.”

Alice turned toward him, sat up straighter, and hammered it home. “Gus. Truly. I appreciate you way more than you can ever imagine.”

But he could imagine. He felt the same way. More than the same way. A lot more. He had always understood the love between them as uneven, as something he carried more heavily than she did. But sitting there beside her, hearing her say all of that, he wasn’t sure anymore. Maybe she felt it as well. Maybe she wanted him too.

Alice leaned her head on Gus’ shoulder, a rare physical display of affection toward him, and this time, he let himself pull her into a long embrace. It would have been wrong not to. The moment deserved it.

On that day, and many times over the years, Gus felt like Alice’s number one—her “day one,” as she liked to call him—but only in the spaces other people left open. When she was seeing James, Gus got phone calls from Alice when James was away coaching games. He always thought that was strange. You only call your boy best friend when your boyfriend is out of town? But then he would explain it away. Of course that was when she had time. Of course that was when long phone calls made sense.

When their calls went from once every couple of weeks to a couple of times a week, he knew something was up. Sure enough, Alice had ended things with James. Then it happened again. The calls stretched farther and farther apart, this time because she had found a new best friend in Hannah. And Gus was happy for her. He really was. Better that she makes new friends and live her life in person than spend all her time at home and on the phone.

But then Hannah started dating someone, and suddenly Alice had time again. The calls returned. The closeness returned, this time with the idea of a summer trip. And then, just as quickly, another opportunity appeared—and Alice took it.

Were Gus and Alice’s plans anywhere near concrete? No. Did she owe him anything? No. Did she have to tell him how many days off she wanted to take, or when? Absolutely not. But after months of dreaming about seeing the world together, did he feel like he deserved to be kept at least a little bit in the loop?

Yeah. He did.

Also looming in the background was her move from Lexington back to the Milwaukee area—closer to home, closer to her family, closer to them. Alice’s promotion and relocation back to Wisconsin had been in the works for a while, and both of them had been looking forward to it. Gus missed his best friend. After so much distance, Alice was finally moving back within reach.

The call came bright and early a couple of weeks before the California call, just as Gus was getting ready to leave for work.

“I got the job in Sussex!”

He was filled with pride that she was moving up in the world, and with immense joy that she would once again be close by. But after they hung up, unexpectedly—or perhaps all too knowingly—a quiet dread settled in him. Gus tried to let in only positive thoughts. He really did. Still, he walked the twenty minutes to work beneath a gloomy late-March Chicago sky, with the scent of lake winds and a brewing storm hanging in the air, unable to shake a lingering sense of foreboding and unease.

In the couple of weeks after her trip to California, Alice and Gus’ texts grew sparse, and he found himself increasingly anxious about her permanent return to southern Wisconsin, now only a month or so away. Alice reached out once to ask about his visit to Philadelphia and how it had been spending time with his family. Gus answered with details, but not with his usual abundance. There was a formality in his replies, a carefulness he could not quite hide.

Try as he might, he was still upset. He wanted to reach back out with his whole heart, to return to the ease they had always known, but he couldn’t. They had never been big texters anyway—mostly callers—so the silence did not feel entirely unnatural. Gus was also fairly certain Alice hadn’t thought much about their last phone call, especially coming off the high of her trip.

They were going to be fine.

It was a Tuesday, and spirits were high. Summer finally felt like it was here to stay. The experiments he had set up the night before had come back promising. Then his phone buzzed.

Tuesday 3:54 PM

Gussss

Questionknn”

Alice was making her last visit to Wisconsin for work and apartment hunting before the move. Gus had known she was in the area for a couple of days—the location tag under her name had said Brookfield, WI—and though he didn’t want to admit it, he had been waiting for her to text.

He responded immediately.

hello! whats good”

uh u are in wisconsin??

What are your plans for today?

just work and heading to the gym, so nothing

Welll I wanna see you and I’m in wisco

im down!! im free whenever

Wait. Was he? Gus had been planning to stay late and analyze the results that had just come in, then head to the gym afterward. He had only recently started going again after the months-long hiatus leading up to quals, and some part of him felt disappointed by how quickly he had been willing to drop everything just to see her.

wait actually i just remembered my afternoon meeting tmr got moved up to the morning so i might have some work things to sort out tonight

It was true, but it was also an excuse. Gus felt an immediate gut punch: the promise of a long hug after months apart, of that unparalleled warmth and comfort of her embrace, taken away just like that—and by his own hand. Still, beneath the disappointment, he couldn’t help feeling proud of himself.

but its line dancing night at red rock, im sure vanessa and noah will be there and i think lauren should be free to hang out too. ill join when im done with work.”

I work early tomorrow morning so I’m going to drive back a little early tonight.”

Gus worked late. He went to the gym. He went home. He never showed.

On her drive back to her hotel, Alice texted.

Tuesday 10:59 PM

Did I do something?

Wednesday 1:27 PM

Hey sorry, just got done with the meeting and lunch. How I learned about your trip to California upset me. It still does. I would like space, indefinitely.

Wednesday 1:27 PM

You stopped sharing location with Alice Quinn.

Okay sounds good well then I will respect that. Edited.

Really, what Gus wanted to say was:

Hey, sorry. Just got done with the meeting and lunch—though really, I needed time to find the words. Right or wrong, the way I learned about your trip to California upset me. And as happy as I am that you had an amazing time out there, that you got to see a little more of this great big world we had talked so much about exploring, it still does.

As much happiness and joy and love—so much love—as you bring into my life, the lows are just as overwhelming, if not more. They are utterly paralyzing. With you, I know I will once again experience heights I had never known before. But I also expect, as I have so many times in the past, to be cast aside and made to feel smaller than I am. And the longer I prolong whatever it is we have, the more afraid I become that one day, I won’t recover from it.

So I think I need space. Indefinitely.

I’m sorry.

This time, Gus really thought it was over. Most of him hoped so. A small part of him didn’t. But with that, at least he knew Alice wouldn’t text or call. If they ever spoke again, it would be on his terms.

A wave of freedom and relief washed over him. He closed his eyes, allowing the tears to well but not spill, then turned back to the two monitors before him, both bearing the promise of a publication. He returned to work—numb, wounded, and, somehow, proud.
